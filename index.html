Albert Einstein

Albert Einstein was a German-born physicist who revolutionized science with his theory of relativity. His most famous equation, E=mc2, established the relationship between mass and energy, fundamentally changing how we understand physics. In 1905, Einstein introduced the special theory of relativity, which demonstrated that the speed of light is constant and that the laws of physics apply equally to all non-accelerating observers. A decade later, he expanded this to the general theory of relativity, showing how massive objects warp space-time, which we experience as gravity.

Einstein's work had a profound impact on physics and cosmology. He won the Nobel Prize in 1921 for his explanation of the photoelectric effect, a key contribution to quantum theory. Fleeing Nazi Germany in 1933, he settled in the U.S. and continued his work at Princeton. Though a pacifist, Einstein's warning about Germany's atomic ambitions helped spark the Manhattan Project, though he did not participate directly. Beyond science, Einstein was a humanitarian and advocate for civil rights, leaving a legacy that extended well beyond his groundbreaking discoveries.
